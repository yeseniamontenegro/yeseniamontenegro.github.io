SAT-optional applications are changing UMD admissions

By Yesenia Montenegro

At the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, dozens of universities across the country no longer required SAT or ACT score submissions for the 2021, 2022 and 2023 school years.

Since 2021, prospective students have the option to decide whether or not they want their SAT or ACT scores to be considered when applying to the University of Maryland. Because many SAT and ACT testing dates were canceled or postponed during the height of the pandemic, many colleges have adopted test-optional applications, or plan to de-emphasize test scores when reviewing undergraduate applications. Now, applications to UMD are evaluated “holistically,” meaning that all parts of an application are analyzed, no matter if an applicant submits a test score or not, according to the university’s written admission policy.

Changing scores

A review of standardized testing submissions when required shows little variance, but when optional testing is submitted, the average scores appear to be higher.

From 2017 to 2020, the average SAT score prospective students submitted with their application was between 1375 and 1380.

In 2021 however, when SAT scores became optional, the average was 1430.


This raises the question-: Do test-optional applications allow more students to be admitted, because only those with high SAT scores are submitting results, or are more students being admitted without their scores being considered?

According to university research platform Cappex, a score of 1400 on the SAT places a student in the 95th percentile, meaning they have scored higher than 95% of test takers. The average score at UMD is now even higher than the 95th percentile.

This could also mean that if and/or when UMD decides to require test scores again, the minimum they are looking for may be higher. The average SAT score in 2021 is 55 points higher than in previous years, which may not seem like a lot, but can make a significant difference when applying to colleges. Now that the average is so much higher, and will probably stay at a higher score in 2022 and 2023, getting accepted to UMD has the potential to be more difficult in the future.

Admission numbers

Data shows that there are already significant differences in application and admission numbers. In 2021, UMD received more than 50,000 applications, up from the 32,000 received in 2020. However, only 40% of applicants were admitted in 2021, while 51% were admitted in 2020. While there is no evidence that there is a direct correlation between the two, it is interesting to note that significantly more students were admitted when the average SAT score was lower and submission was required.

Freshman Krissy Verma, a kinesiology major, submitted her SAT score to UMD, but not to two other schools she applied to. She felt her score was high enough for UMD, but might not have been enough for some of her other options.

Verma started studying for the exam three months in advance, and spent at least two hours a day studying, she said. Because of how much effort went into it, she thinks it still made sense to submit her score. However, since a lot of students might not have had the opportunity to fully prepare for the exam, or even take it because of the pandemic, test-optional was a fair way of admitting students, she said.

According to Kaplan, a test prep service, students study for about 10 to 20 hours a week when preparing for the SAT. With so much time being put in, deciding whether or not to submit scores can be a tough decision. (Photo: Yesenia Montenegro)

UMD Freshman Shreeya Patel, who applied in 2021, decided to submit her SAT score, despite there being no requirement.

“I had already spent a lot of time studying and my score wasn’t that bad. It was in the range of scores that UMD requires, so I went ahead and submitted it anyway because I had invested so much time already,” Patel said.

Even though she submitted her score, Patel likes that it is not required. She feels that a test score should not be the basis on which admission should be decided, because it doesn’t necessarily show how well a student can perform in higher education, she said.
